## **Those Who Can't** (any more) teach

It was 2008 when I went in to work one day at what was then the world's largest independent pharmaceutical ad agency, AbelsonTaylor in Chicago. This gig does not appear on my resume, as I never completed a job for them.  
But that day, as producer on a massive, interactive site shilling for a new antacid, I found that the others at my level on the project —Client Rep and Art Director— were no longer with the company. I wondered how long it would be before I'd follow them.  

The same week I applied for unemployment was a record setter, with a great percentage of unemployed americans than any time since the Great Depression. Welcome to the burst of the "housing bubble"... 

High-end interactive marketing had no established ROI. Money for such projects dried instantly up.  
Rather than go into designing _banner ads_ —the design bane of the Net in those days— I took a trip to Australia, to compose for and appear in (as a faun) _The Bacchae_.  
I drank, appropriately, too much wine and, on my return to the states, started looking for teaching gigs, just to see if I'd be any good at it.  
I'd always thought I might become a college professor in my retirement... The call just came a little earlier than anticipated.  

A couple semesters with the Art Institute of Illinois would have turned me off the prospect forever, except for a single data point:

> In student evals, I scored far higher than the institutional average.

I thought, beyond any sense I had of my own poor ability, I might be onto something.

## **Tecnológico de Monterrey**, the _Yale of Mexico_

Former Vice President Al Gore had just, rather absurdly, dubbed _El Tec_ with that monicker (it's much more that country's SUNY system) so I gladly accepted an offer to spend 2013 teaching interactive multimedia in Guadalajara, and the subsequent offer to do the same at the flagship campus in Monterrey. It was there I created what may well have been the first college course in VR specifically targeting <a href="https://time.com/3901393/google-cardboard-iphone/" target="_blank"><i>Google Cardboard</i></a>, released in 2014.  

> It was here I was first "professor" —_de Ambinetes Virtuales_. It felt good.

I found myself very comfortable as an _expat_, having always considered myself a citizen of the world. Friends I made, relationships established with persons from around the globe, convinced me that the time had come to see more of the old place. My passport became my... _passport_ —to a wider, more interesting life.

It was only later that I realized teaching had very possibly saved me: Advertising, interactive marketing had been 60 hour weeks minimum, in-office. Most of it spent sitting at a computer. Abroad, I grew healthier, happier, more relaxed. I found time to make art, music and lasting friendships.

## **Korea (the Good One)** and the East

Next I spent two years entirely in the Eastern Hemisphere. Living in S. Korea, I vacationed in Australia and Japan. Tho I never set foot in it (visas, at that time, were made out for _seven years, min_ —rather expensive for the visit of just a month or so) I felt the deep, gravitational pull of China. Many people I worked with at SOL International School went on to speak fluent Mandarin.

This was the "Little Rocket Man" era of U.S. / N. Korean relations and among the global _expatria_ clustered around Seoul the conversation turned to bug-out plans for when the (ironically) _inevitable_ arrived.  
I said I'd get out on the roof of my building with the _kimchi_ pots and watch the bombs come down: "Cause I never seen _nothin_ like that!"  

_And then what?_ Friends wanted to know. I assured them I reckoned the next thing I'd do was _die_.  

But it was during a dry run, national emergency drill, that I learned what Koreans thought  of it all. While many of my peers huddled, joking, in the shelters, I was up on that roof, watching a guy sweep the sidewalk in front of his shop in the streets below.  
When the sirens wend off, all over the city (the whole of the lower peninsula, geopolitical _island_) he didn't miss a beat, kept right on sweeping.  
Barring the accidents to which all flesh is heir, I imagine he's there still, sweeping away.

## **SUNY Polytechnic** and Game Design  

Back in the states, all this gelled into Game Design. A two-year contract, I knew from the first I'd never apply for renewal. I was looking for a home, not a job for which I had to periodically reapply. I figured this meant something like _tenure track_.  
It was kind of unfortunate, then, that I fell in love with Little Falls, New York —a city of supposedly 5000 souls, where I lived across from the cemetery.  
I might have stayed there forever. The house I rented was for sale the whole time, and easily within my budget! Except for the recurring hat-in-hand, professionally, I think I'd still be contentedly commuting the 40 minutes to Utica in that beat up old van.
